GEORGE N. TIDD

PRESIDENT AND CHAIRMAN OF AMERICAN GAS AND ELECTRIC (1923-1949)



THE EFFICIENCY EXPERT


The company’s longest-serving president, George N. Tidd, began his career at Waverly and Sayre Electric Company, a small electric company on the New York-Pennsylvania border. In 1904, he was discovered by Richard Breed and offered a management position at the Marion Light and Heat Company, a utility held by the Electric Company of America (ECA).


When ECA became American Gas and Electric (AGE) in 1906, Tidd rose through the ranks from general manager to president.


Tidd championed expansion and efficiency. “In our work we have opportunity for efficient organization in labor and material unparalleled,” he wrote. To achieve this unparalleled efficiency, he compared operations across companies, systematically identifying where improvements could be made.


Tidd also saw the importance of building a network of transmission lines in order to increase reliability and serve more customers. During the 1920s, thousands of miles of transmission lines were built in AGE’s service areas in Ohio, Indiana, Michigan, Pennsylvania, New Jersey, Virginia, North Carolina, Kentucky and West Virginia. 


As general manager, Tidd created an emphasis on employees as members of a family by sharing important news in a companywide publication and welcoming their feedback on the company’s operations.
