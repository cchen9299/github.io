SAMUEL INSULL

President of Western Edison Light Company(1892 - 1929)


THE VISIONARY


A protégé of Thomas Edison, Samuel Insull is one of the most influential figures in the origin of the electrical utilities industry. Insull immigrated to the United States in 1881 and helped establish Edison General Electric (later General Electric), becoming vice president in 1889.


In 1892, Insull moved to Chicago and led Chicago Edison Company and later led Commonwealth Edison Company. He created a network of holding companies that provided utility service to nearly 5,000 communities in 32 states. His subsidiaries included Michigan Gas and Electric Company and Public Service of Oklahoma, setting the groundwork for much of what would later become American Electric Power (AEP).


In opposition to many of his colleagues, Insull argued that monopolies were good for the industry because they led to government oversight. A single large utility company would serve more customers, he said, and would lead to lower prices for customers and the construction of larger plants. He also believed that centralized generation allowed for efficient expansion into rural communities. 


Insull’s business model was emulated by utility companies throughout the United States, including one of his largest rivals: American Gas and Electric.


During the Great Depression, Insull faced federal prosecution for mail fraud and violations of the Bankruptcy Act. Although he was never convicted, his experiences led to business and banking reforms that continue to affect how utility companies handle investments and shareholder relationships.
